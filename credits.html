About This Project

Behind the scenes, there was a lot of work to turn messy numbers from different sources into clean, comparable, and meaningful insights.

We took data from trusted sources like FAOSTAT, EFSA, and the FAO. Some of it was easy to download. Others — like food prices — had to be scraped from websites . Once collected, we cleaned everything up, removed the irrelevant bits, and organized it all into neat tables.

Every source had its own way of naming food. So, to match "beef" with "cow meat", we used a mix of smart algorithms and a powerful vocabulary from the FAO called AGROVOC. This helped us connect the dots and make sure everything meant the same thing across datasets. When tech couldn’t do it, we fixed it by hand. Because accuracy matters.

We then filtered the data to focus on what’s relevant: solid foods with a significant environmental footprint. We also removed things like bottled water. This helped us build clear, readable charts and gave structure to the final interactive experience.

And yes, we did it twice. Once to get a solid base with Italy’s most consumed foods. Then again, with more items, to spot patterns like whether eco-friendly food is also cheaperand to power the game at the end.
